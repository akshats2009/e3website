Grant Help & Tips

Applying for a grant, step by step

Grants are free money you don't pay back — but they're competitive, and the application process trips a lot of people up. Use these tips to put together a strong application, and the directory below to get free, expert help when you need a real person.

Find grants with our AI finder →

How to write a strong grant application

  1. Confirm you're eligible first. Read the eligibility rules carefully before spending time on an application. Check business type (for-profit vs. 501(c)(3) nonprofit), location, size, revenue, time in operation, and any owner requirements. Applying when you don't qualify is the #1 wasted effort.
  2. Read the full guidelines — twice. Funders publish exactly what they fund, what they won't fund, and how they score applications. Mirror their language and priorities in your answers.
  3. Tell a clear, specific story. Explain who you are, the problem you're solving, who benefits, and what you'll do with the money. Concrete details and real numbers beat buzzwords.
  4. Build a realistic budget. Show exactly how the funds will be used and tie each line item back to your goals. If a match is required, plan for it early.
  5. Gather your documents ahead of time. Common asks: EIN, business registration, financial statements, a 501(c)(3) determination letter (nonprofits), a SAM.gov / UEI number (federal grants), and owner ID. Keep a folder ready so deadlines don't sneak up on you.
  6. Tailor every application. Don't copy-paste one essay everywhere. Reuse your core story, but answer each funder's actual questions in their words.
  7. Never miss the deadline. Submit a day or two early — portals get slow and crash near cutoffs. Note whether rolling grants review monthly or quarterly.
  8. Proofread, then have someone else read it. Typos and unclear answers cost real points. A fresh set of eyes catches what you can't.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Applying when you don't meet the stated eligibility criteria.
  • Missing the deadline — or submitting at the last minute.
  • Ignoring the funder's priorities and writing what you wish they funded.
  • A vague or unrealistic budget with no detail.
  • Reusing a generic application instead of answering the actual questions.
  • Forgetting required attachments (financials, tax status, registrations).
  • Skipping the proofread and final review.
  • Applying to only one grant — apply to several to improve your odds.

Where to get free help

You don't have to do this alone. These organizations offer free, expert help finding funding and preparing applications — most are government-funded or nonprofit.

SBA Local Assistance & SBDCs

Small Business Development Centers offer free advising on funding, business plans, and grant readiness, in every state.

Find your local office →

SCORE

Free, confidential mentoring from experienced business volunteers — including help reviewing grant applications. Online or in person.

Get a free mentor →

Women's Business Centers

SBA-funded centers that help women entrepreneurs with funding, training, and counseling at low or no cost.

Find a center →

Veterans Business Outreach Centers

Free business and funding help for veterans, service members, and military spouses starting or growing a business.

Find a VBOC →

MBDA Business Centers

The Minority Business Development Agency helps minority-owned businesses access capital, contracts, and grant opportunities.

Find an MBDA center →

Grants.gov Support

The federal grants portal. Its support desk answers questions about applying for federal grants — call 1-800-518-4726 or email support@grants.gov.

Grants.gov help →

Candid (Foundation Directory)

The go-to research tool for nonprofit grants and foundations. Use it free at a nearby Funding Information Network library.

Dial 211 (United Way)

A free, confidential helpline that connects you to local programs, financial assistance, and community resources. Call or text 211.
