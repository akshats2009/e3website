Beyond Restaurants, Boba Cafes, and Souvenir Shops: The Historical and Modern Economic Solidarity of Chinatown

By Minghao Gu · February 2, 2026

A blur of red and gold danced throughout the streets of Chinese Lunar New Year. The Chinese Dragon moves as one body, yet are held by a collective—symbolism of the values the community share together. Chinatown, home to 150 years of immigrants, is a symbol of Chinese heritage and identity. Yet, Chinatown is much more than just a destination for tourists; Chinatown is a beacon for economic solidarity.

History

The first notable wave of Chinese immigrants came in the late 1840s during the California Gold Rush, with the population rising from approximately 7,500 in 1850 to more than 100,000 in 1880. These immigrants performed manual labor for the mining and railroad industries. To escape the lack of jobs in their country and the chaos of the Opium Wars, Chinese people moved to America in hopes of a better life.

Yet, despite a history of hard work, Chinese immigrants were faced with discrimination. Deniz Kearney’s Working Party rallied support for the destruction of Chinese businesses with the slogan, “The Chinese Must Go.” The 1882 Chinese Exclusion Act—the first and only law implemented to prevent all members of a specific ethnic or national group from immigrating to the United States—was the pinnacle of Chinese racism in the 19th century.

In an attempt to achieve solidarity in the face of everyday racism, Chinese immigrants built Chinatown, which became the most important factor for economic survival. The first sizable Chinatown was established in San Francisco during the 1850s, serving as a hub for those seeking safety and work. For example, during the gold rush, Chinese immigrants faced heavy taxes and violence in the mines. In response, they opened laundries and restaurants within Chinatown, allowing them to earn a living without relying on white-owned businesses.

Similar to how San Francisco's Chinatown created economic solidarity, New York City’s Chinatown was another epitome of Chinese culture. In 1890, the streets of NYC Chinatown were filled with joss houses, curio shops, and Chinese restaurants. Even when faced with the Geary Act of 1892—which required Chinese residents to carry internal passports—economic solidarity helped the community persevere. By 1900, New York’s Chinatown became a thriving district, where Chinese immigrants established their own schools, theaters, and temples.

Modern

Chinatown isn’t complacent, constantly evolving to fit the needs of each generation. For example, the youth cultural workers of San Francisco's Chinatown have created new events like the Chinatown Music Festival and night markets. These initiatives highlight the modern creativity within the community while continuing to draw in visitors to support local businesses. Through youth advocacy, Chinatown has also seen an increase in affordable housing and preservation of historic landmarks.

However, the story of new innovation isn’t tied to San Francisco. All around America, Chinatown’s are rapidly changing for the new generation. Seattle’s Chinatown has modern exhibition spaces and galleries that showcase Asian-American art, bridging the gap between historical heritage and contemporary expression. In New York, younger generations are revitalizing traditional tea shops and bakeries, blending classic recipes with modern branding to appeal to a wider audience.

Amidst the changes Chinatown undergoes, structural elements remain intact to continue Chinatown’s legacy as a community built upon solidarity. Chinatowns today are filled with family associations and benevolent organizations that have existed for over a century. These groups provide social services, financial aid, and linguistic support, ensuring that newcomers have the resources they need to succeed in a new environment.

In a similar fashion, Chinatown remains a significant anchor in cultural preservation. Streets are filled with parades, lit up by red lanterns, and enriched with the aroma of traditional cuisine. These cultural landmarks serve as a reminder of the community’s resilience and the importance of maintaining one's heritage. By preserving these traditions, Chinatown ensures that future generations can connect with their roots while building a prosperous future.

150 years later, the same blur of red and yellow dances through the streets of Chinese Lunar New Year. The same red lanterns glow outside houses, while mahjong tiles clatter in crowded apartments. Beyond the surface of restaurants, boba cafes, and souvenir shops, Chinatown remains a sanctuary for those seeking a place to call home—a community built on 150 years of economic solidarity.
